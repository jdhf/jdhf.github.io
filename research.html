Hα survey of SFCGs

Narrow-band image (left-side panel), net Hα image (centered panel) and NUV-band (right-side panel) images of SFCG280. Yellow circles enclose the UV-bright candidate members and the white circle is the unique UV-faint close galaxy with known z.

We derive and describe the first Hα observations and star formation activity of 11 SFCGs. Those groups present the largest physical concentrations of star-formation activity in the Local Universe.

Star-Forming Compact Groups (SFCGs)

Scheme which summarizes and compares the typical characteristic evolutionary stage, fraction of star-forming galaxies and physical size of the three group samples compared here. The vertical axis depicts an evolutionary sequence (left axis) in which the proxy is considered the surplus in crossing time with respect to the HCG linear fit Δlog(H0tc) (right axis), see Eq. 8 of HFMdO16. We describe the first sample (z<0.15) of compact groups dominated by star-forming galaxies. This distinctive feature comes from the fact that the group selection was applied over catalogues of GALEX UV galaxy sources. A sub-sample of 40 SFCGs shows median group properties clearly distinct from other compact group samples studied up to now: low velocity dispersions ∼120 km s-1, low virial masses ∼3.5·1012 Msun and a high star-forming fraction fs·f∼95%.

Galaxy Populations in the Cluster Phase-Space

Stripping intensity. Isocontours of stripping intensity η= Pram/Πgal overplotted in the CPPSs of the halo populations in the case of snap(r200)=63 (top) and the galaxy populations (bottom). Each contour is labelled by the ratio of ram-pressure over the anchoring pressure of gas in the Milky Way η (see equation 1 and corresponding text for details) with this set of values η=(0.1,0.3,1,3,10). The rest of elements of the figure are defined in the same way as Fig. 1 of HF+14

This work pointed out the main elements that modulate the observed phase-space (radial and velocity) distribution of passive and star-forming galaxies: (1) the uncertainty in the derivation of the celestial position of cluster center, (2) the quenching in the galaxy star formation produced by the ram-pressure stripping and (3) the segregation in the cluster phase-space between accreted and non-accreted galaxy haloes depending on the radius and the look-back time in which those galaxies are considered accreted. This work also provided me knowledge in the dynamics of the different galaxy populations in galaxy clusters.
